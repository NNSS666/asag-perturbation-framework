  2.1 Automated Short Answer Grading: Models and Benchmarks

3. The Gap This Research Fills [transition paragraph]

**Section 2.1 — ASAG Models and Benchmarks (~500-800 words):**
- Open with Burrows et al. (2015) as the anchor survey establishing the five eras of ASAG
- Trace the trajectory: feature-based (pre-2015) -> ML on handcrafted features (2015-2017) -> deep learning (2017-2019) -> transformer fine-tuning (2019-2022) -> LLM zero-shot (2023-present)
- For each era, cite 2-3 representative papers from the annotated bibliography
- Highlight the benchmark convergence on SemEval 2013 Task 7 (Dzikovska et al., 2013) and its limitations (single dataset, clean inputs only)
- End with the observation that evaluation methodology has not kept pace with model sophistication — accuracy, F1, and QWK remain the dominant metrics despite known limitations (EDM 2023 findings if available)

**Section 2.2 — Adversarial and Perturbation-Based Evaluation (~400-600 words):**
- Open with the broader NLP adversarial robustness movement (TextAttack, CheckList, etc.)
- Narrow to educational AI: Filighera et al. (2024) gaming attacks, Ding et al. (2020) garbled inputs, Kumar et al. (2020) adversarial testing of AES
- Highlight that adversarial work on ASAG specifically is sparse — most work targets AES or general NLP
- Note the methodological gap: existing adversarial studies test individual attack types but do not propose a systematic multi-family perturbation PROTOCOL with complementary metrics (IVR, SSR, ASR)

**Section 2.3 — Educational Assessment Validity and Reliability (~400-600 words):**
- Open with the construct validity framework (Messick 1989, if cited)
- Discuss Ferrara and Qunbar (2022) on validity arguments for AI-based scoring
- Connect to rubric-based scoring and the distinction between construct-relevant and construct-irrelevant variance
- End with the observation that validity frameworks exist in educational measurement theory but have not been operationalized into systematic perturbation-test protocols for ASAG

**Gap Statement (~200-300 words):**
- Use the draft gap paragraph from RESEARCH.md as a starting point (see "Gap Statement Draft" section)
- Refine it based on the actual papers found and annotated
- The paragraph should explicitly state: (1) what the field does now (clean-test evaluation with QWK), (2) what is missing (perturbation-based construct validity testing), (3) what this paper provides (three-family perturbation protocol with IVR, SSR, ASR metrics)

Writing guidelines:
- Synthesize, don't enumerate. Each paragraph should make a point supported by multiple citations, not list papers one by one.
- Use Harvard in-text citation format: (Author, Year) or Author (Year). NOT numbered references [1].
- Use hedging language appropriately: "suggests", "indicates", "demonstrates" rather than "proves".
- Keep total word count between 1500-2500 words (IFKAD full papers are 3000-5000 words, so Related Work should be ~30-40% of the paper).
- Write in formal academic English, third person.
