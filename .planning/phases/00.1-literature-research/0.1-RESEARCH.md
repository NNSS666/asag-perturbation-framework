## Summary

This phase establishes the bibliographic foundation for the IFKAD 2026 paper by Ferdinando Sasso and Andrea De Mauro. The paper argues that standard agreement metrics (QWK, accuracy) are insufficient for evaluating ASAG reliability and proposes a perturbation-based evaluation framework. The literature review must position this claim within three converging bodies of work: (1) ASAG methodology and benchmarks, (2) adversarial/perturbation evaluation of NLP and educational AI, and (3) educational assessment validity and reliability theory.

The core gap to be articulated is this: the ASAG field has matured in terms of accuracy benchmarks (SemEval, SRA datasets, transformer fine-tuning) but has no established methodology for evaluating whether a grading model's score changes are driven by genuine understanding or surface artifacts. The adversarial/perturbation literature from NLP exists but focuses primarily on attack robustness in general NLP settings rather than educational grading contexts. Educational assessment validity theory (construct validity, rubric-based scoring) provides the theoretical vocabulary but has not been operationalized into a systematic perturbation test protocol for ASAG systems. This paper fills that intersection.

The search strategy should cover seven target journals (IJAIED, Computers & Education, Assessment in Education, British Journal of Educational Technology, Language Testing, Applied Measurement in Education, Educational Measurement: Issues and Practice / Journal of Educational Measurement) using Google Scholar, Web of Science, ERIC, and Semantic Scholar, filtered to 2015-2026 with journal-article type restrictions. Conference papers from ACL, EMNLP, BEA, AIED, and EDM should be included only when no equivalent journal publication exists and the work is foundational (e.g., the SemEval 2013 Task 7 shared task paper, or Devlin et al. 2019 BERT).

**Primary recommendation:** Execute the literature search in three parallel streams (ASAG methods, adversarial/perturbation evaluation, educational validity theory), maintain a Zotero library with a shared collection per theme, annotate each entry with journal name + year + relevance tag, and draft the literature review section with an explicit "gap this paper fills" paragraph before any code is written.

### Pattern 1: Systematic Search Protocol (PRISMA-Lite)

**What:** A structured, reproducible search using Boolean queries across multiple databases with documented inclusion/exclusion criteria.

**When to use:** For LIT-01, LIT-02, LIT-03 — any search that will be reported in the paper's methodology section.

**Example search strings (Web of Science / Google Scholar):**

```
Stream 1: ASAG
("automatic short answer grading" OR "automated short answer grading" OR "automatic short answer scoring")
AND (year:[2015 TO 2026])
Filter: Article type = Journal Article

Stream 2: Adversarial/Perturbation Evaluation
("adversarial" OR "perturbation" OR "robustness")
AND ("automated scoring" OR "automatic grading" OR "essay scoring" OR "short answer grading")
AND (year:[2015 TO 2026])
Filter: Article type = Journal Article (preferred)

Stream 3: Educational Assessment Validity
("construct validity" OR "reliability" OR "rubric" OR "scoring rubric")
AND ("automated assessment" OR "automated scoring" OR "automated grading")
AND (year:[2015 TO 2026])
Filter: Article type = Journal Article
```

**Inclusion criteria:**
- Peer-reviewed journal articles (primary) or highly cited conference papers where no journal equivalent exists
- English language
- Publication year 2015-2026
- Topic directly relevant to one of the three streams

**Exclusion criteria:**
- Non-peer-reviewed (preprints accepted only if the work is otherwise unreachable)
- Workshop papers with fewer than 10 citations (unless very recent, < 2023)
- Papers focused on essay scoring of continuous prose (distinct problem from short answer grading) unless they address validity/adversarial issues transferable to ASAG

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Conference-heavy bibliography:** IFKAD reviewers expect journal-dominant citations. Every conference paper cited should be audited: is there a journal version? If not, is it foundational enough to justify inclusion?
- **Citation dumping without synthesis:** Listing papers per theme without explaining how they relate to each other or to the gap is a common weakness. Synthesize, don't enumerate.
- **Ignoring the educational assessment literature:** The NLP/AI community tends to cite only NLP venues. The gap in the paper is partly an educational measurement argument (construct validity). Citing Ferrara 2022 (Journal of Educational Measurement) or similar validates the methodological framing.
- **Over-reliance on arXiv preprints:** For IFKAD, published journal articles establish credibility. Use arXiv only when (a) the paper is seminal (e.g., BERT, SBERT) and widely cited, or (b) a journal version is pending.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Conflating AES (Automated Essay Scoring) with ASAG

**What goes wrong:** A large body of automated scoring literature focuses on long-form essay scoring (AES), not short answer grading. AES and ASAG have different properties — AES measures writing quality and fluency; ASAG measures factual/conceptual accuracy of short answers. Including AES papers without distinguishing the differences dilutes the related work section.

**Why it happens:** Google Scholar searches for "automated scoring" return predominantly AES papers (Turnitin, e-rater, GMAT/GRE scoring) which vastly outnumber ASAG papers.

**How to avoid:** Use "short answer grading" or "short answer scoring" as required terms in searches. When including AES papers (e.g., for validity arguments, adversarial robustness methodology), explicitly note the difference and explain why the finding transfers.

**Warning signs:** If more than 30% of bibliography entries are AES papers, the search strategy has drifted.

### Pitfall 2: Missing Journal Versions of Conference Papers

**What goes wrong:** Many ASAG papers first appear as conference papers (ACL, AIED, EDM) and are later extended into journal articles. Citing the conference version when a journal version exists weakens the bibliography.

**Why it happens:** Google Scholar and Semantic Scholar often surface the conference version first because it has more citations (older).

**How to avoid:** For every conference paper, search the author name + title in Google Scholar, sort by date descending, and check for a journal version within 1-2 years.

**Warning signs:** More than 5 conference papers in the bibliography that were published before 2023 without checking for journal follow-ups.

### Pitfall 3: No Gap Statement Before Code

**What goes wrong:** Literature research finishes with an annotated bibliography but no explicit articulation of the research gap. The gap statement is left to be written during paper drafting (Phase 6), by which point the literature has been forgotten.

**Why it happens:** Researchers assume they will remember the gap once they have read the papers. They don't.

**How to avoid:** LIT-05 requires a draft literature review section that includes the gap statement. Write this section before Phase 1 begins, even if it is rough and will be revised later.

**Warning signs:** LIT-05 deliverable is "annotated bibliography" only, with no draft prose.

### Pitfall 4: Ignoring Educational Measurement Journals

**What goes wrong:** The paper is submitted to an NLP-adjacent venue but the methodology relies on educational assessment validity concepts (construct validity, rubric reliability). Without citations from educational measurement journals, reviewers from that community will not accept the framing.

**Why it happens:** The author's primary expertise is NLP/ML, so literature searching is biased toward ACL, EMNLP, and IJAIED.

**How to avoid:** Dedicate explicit search effort to Journal of Educational Measurement, Applied Measurement in Education, and Assessment in Education. Even 3-5 well-chosen citations from these journals anchors the validity argument.

**Warning signs:** Zero citations from educational measurement journals (non-IJAIED) in the bibliography.

### Pitfall 5: IFKAD Citation Style Non-Compliance

**What goes wrong:** References are formatted in APA or ACL style rather than Harvard, which the IFKAD submission guidelines explicitly require.

**Why it happens:** Academic default in NLP/AI is APA 7th or ACL style.

**How to avoid:** Configure Zotero to use a Harvard CSL style from the start. Verify format against IFKAD template before finalizing. Harvard style has specific rules for in-text citations (Author, Year) and reference list ordering (alphabetical by first author surname).

**Warning signs:** In-text citations using parenthetical (Author et al., Year) — this is APA format, not Harvard. Harvard uses (Author et al., Year) too, but there are differences in the reference list formatting (e.g., place of publication required for books; journal volume/issue formatting differs).

## State of the Art

### ASAG Research Trajectory

| Era | Approach | Representative Work | When |
|-----|----------|---------------------|------|
| Feature-based | Lexical overlap, LSA, knowledge graphs | Mohler & Mihalcea (2009, EACL) | Pre-2015 |
| ML on hand-crafted features | SVM, regression on linguistic features | Heilman & Madnani (2015, BEA workshop) | 2015-2017 |
| Deep learning | CNNs, RNNs, attention | Riordan, Horbach et al. (2017, BEA) | 2017-2019 |
| Transformer fine-tuning | BERT, RoBERTa fine-tuned on SemEval | Sung et al. (2019), multiple | 2019-2022 |
| Current: LLM zero/few-shot | GPT-4, Gemini, Llama as zero-shot graders | Multiple 2023-2024 papers | 2023-present |

**Key current state (2026):** The ASAG field has converged on transformer-based graders fine-tuned per question or per domain, supplemented by emerging LLM-based zero-shot approaches. Evaluation remains primarily on SemEval 2013 Task 7 (Dzikovska et al., 2013) using accuracy, F1, and QWK. No standardized perturbation-based robustness evaluation protocol exists.

### Adversarial Evaluation of NLP/Educational AI

| Old Approach | Current Approach | When Changed | Impact |
|--------------|-----------------|--------------|--------|
| Clean test-set accuracy only | Adversarial examples + robustness metrics | 2018-2020 in general NLP | AES robustness now studied; ASAG adversarial work still sparse |
| Attack success rate (ASR) borrowed from vision | Task-appropriate adversarial metrics (IVR, SSR) | Not yet established for ASAG — this paper's contribution | Gap this paper fills |
| General NLP adversarial (TextAttack, PWWS) | Domain-specific adversarial tailored to ASAG | Filighera et al. 2024 | More realistic educational gaming attacks |

**Deprecated/outdated:**
- QWK as the sole ASAG evaluation metric: EDM 2023 paper (Evaluation of QWK as Standard) demonstrates its insufficiency; the field is moving toward multi-metric evaluation.
- Feature-based graders: Still useful as baselines but no longer competitive against fine-tuned transformers on standard benchmarks.

## Gap Statement Draft (for LIT-05)

The following draft paragraph articulates the research gap for use in the paper's related work section. It should be revised once the systematic search is complete.

> Automated Short Answer Grading (ASAG) systems have achieved substantial agreement with human raters on standard benchmark datasets such as SemEval 2013 Task 7 (Dzikovska et al., 2013), yet standard evaluation metrics — accuracy, F1, and quadratic weighted kappa — measure only aggregate agreement on clean test inputs. They do not distinguish between models that grade correctly because they understand the semantic content of a student response and models that grade correctly because they exploit surface-level lexical cues. Filighera et al. (2024) demonstrated that inserting grammatically natural adjectives and adverbs into incorrect student answers causes state-of-the-art BERT and T5 graders to accept them as correct, reducing accuracy by 10-22 percentage points. Ding et al. (2020) showed that even garbled or randomly shuffled inputs could fool content scoring systems. Taken together, these findings suggest that standard evaluation practice systematically overstates the reliability of ASAG systems in deployment conditions. Educational measurement scholarship has established that automated scoring validity requires evidence of construct relevance — that score variation reflects variation in the intended construct, not construct-irrelevant features (Ferrara and Qunbar, 2022; Messick, 1989). No prior work has proposed a systematic, multi-family perturbation protocol that operationalizes construct validity testing for ASAG by simultaneously measuring a system's invariance under meaning-preserving perturbations, sensitivity to meaning-altering perturbations, and resistance to score-gaming perturbations. This paper fills that gap.
